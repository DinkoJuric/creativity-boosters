## ðŸ§  SEGMENT 2: THE FRAMEWORK

*[8 minutes]*

**DINKO:**

Okay, so how do you actually do this?

The SIT Institute developed a five-step process.

**NIKHIL:**

And here's the key â€”

It's not random.

It's systematic.

---

**DINKO:**

**Step one: List all components.**

Take whatever you're working on â€”

A product, a service, a process â€”

And list every part of it.

Don't skip anything.

---

**NIKHIL:**

**Step two: Select an "essential" component to subtract.**

Here's the trick.

Don't pick something trivial.

Pick something you think is **essential**.

Something everyone assumes you need.

---

**DINKO:**

**Step three: Imagine the product without it.**

This is what they call the "virtual product."

What would this thing look like...

Without that component?

---

**NIKHIL:**

**Step four: Identify the "dangling function."**

When you remove something, its function is now missing.

So you ask:

What job was that component doing?

---

**DINKO:**

**Step five: Solve.**

Now you have three options.

**Option A:** Another existing component takes over that function.

**Option B:** The user takes over. Like with Muji or IKEA.

**Option C:** You realize... it wasn't needed at all.

*[Source: SIT Methodology â€” [sitsite.com](https://www.sitsite.com/)]*

---

**NIKHIL:**

Let me give you a concrete example.

The **iPod Shuffle**.

Apple subtracted the screen and the graphical interface.

**DINKO:**

The "dangling function" was navigation.

How do you pick songs without a screen?

**NIKHIL:**

Their answer: shuffle mode.

Just hit play.

Let randomness be the feature.

**DINKO:**

And suddenly you had the cheapest, most gym-friendly iPod ever made.

All because they removed what everyone assumed was essential.

---

**NIKHIL:**

Now, why does this work?

There's a psychological concept called **cognitive fixedness**.

**DINKO:**

It's our brain's tendency to see things only in their current form.

We assume current components are essential...

Because they're there.

**NIKHIL:**

Subtraction forces us to break that assumption.

It makes us question what's really necessary.

And that's where innovation happens.
